Opening Statement

Every major asset class has a standard. Knowledge does not.

Financial assets can be measured, compared, and governed because accounting standards exist that make this possible. Physical assets (machinery, property, infrastructure) are valued, depreciated, and insured according to shared frameworks. Even data is increasingly treated as a governable asset class, with emerging standards for ownership, quality, and provenance.

Knowledge has no equivalent. Despite being the asset on which most consequential decisions depend, knowledge has never been defined with sufficient rigour to be measured, governed, or strategically managed as a unified class. Organisations know this intuitively. They speak of knowledge as critical, irreplaceable, and at risk. Yet they have no common framework for determining what it is, what it is worth, or what happens when it degrades.

This paper argues that this absence is not incidental. It is the central unsolved problem of the knowledge economy, and the reason a new capability has not yet been possible to define, let alone deliver.

That capability is Knowledge Intelligence: the ability to treat everything an organisation knows as a governable, measurable, and decision-relevant asset. Tacit and explicit. Human and documented. Relational and institutional. It has no established category, no recognised framework, and no dominant platform. This paper is its founding statement.

Central to that founding is a prerequisite: before Knowledge Intelligence can be delivered, a Common Knowledge Asset Standard must exist. A shared framework for classifying knowledge across its forms, measuring its confidence and decay, and governing it with the same rigour applied to financial or operational assets. This paper begins to define that standard. Not as a finished answer, but as the open and necessary question that the field must now take seriously.

1 — Knowledge as an Asset Class

Knowledge is broader, deeper, and harder to measure than content alone.

The enterprise has always known that knowledge matters. What it has not developed is a coherent, shared understanding of what knowledge actually is as an asset, in the full sense of the word. Before any intelligence framework can be built, that foundation must be established.

Knowledge exists in two distinct but inseparable forms. Neither is sufficient on its own. Both must be accounted for.

Knowledge expressed in words and numbers represents only the tip of the iceberg. The larger part is tacit: highly personal, hard to formalise, and deeply rooted in action, experience, and individual values.

Ikujiro Nonaka and Hirotaka Takeuchi — The Knowledge-Creating Company, Oxford University Press, 1995
Tacit Knowledge

What organisations know but cannot easily say

Tacit knowledge is the most valuable and the least visible form. It cannot be directly extracted, stored, or transferred in the way a document can. It lives in people, in relationships, in culture, and in practice. Research consistently estimates that tacit knowledge accounts for approximately 80% of an organisation's total knowledge base.Nonaka & Takeuchi

Human expertise and judgement
The capability to make high-quality decisions in complex situations, accumulated through experience and rarely fully articulable.
Relationships and networks
Who trusts whom, who knows whom, and the informal channels through which real decisions are made and real knowledge flows.
Process and institutional memory
Why things are done the way they are done. The accumulated context behind conventions that are rarely written down and often lost when people leave.
Experience and pattern recognition
The ability to recognise situations, anticipate outcomes, and navigate ambiguity based on having navigated it before.
Explicit Knowledge

What organisations know and have expressed

Explicit knowledge is what has been externalised, captured in content, data, and structured form. It is legible, transferable, and the primary focus of the existing enterprise content stack. Yet even this visible layer presents significant governance challenges at scale.

Documents and records
Policies, reports, research, contracts, guidance, and the full range of structured written output produced by the organisation.
Structured data and signals
Quantitative records, system outputs, and operational data that encode organisational activity and performance.
Frameworks and models
The conceptual structures (taxonomies, methodologies, principles) through which the organisation understands and describes itself.
Captured expertise
Explicit articulations of tacit knowledge including playbooks, case studies, and lessons learned, where the organisation has attempted to make expertise transferable.

Treating only explicit knowledge as a measurable asset is like managing a company's finances by counting only the cash on hand. Technically legible, but strategically blind.

2 — The Quantification Challenge

Why knowledge has resisted being treated as an asset until now.

If knowledge is so clearly valuable, why has no common framework for measuring and governing it yet emerged? The answer is not lack of interest. Knowledge, particularly in its tacit forms, presents genuinely hard quantification problems that existing frameworks have not resolved. The scale of the cost is well documented, even if the solutions remain absent.

01

No common unit of measure

Financial assets have currency. Physical assets have weight, volume, and condition. Knowledge has no agreed unit. A decade of domain expertise cannot be straightforwardly compared to a thousand documents. Without a common unit, comparison, governance, and investment decisions become matters of opinion rather than evidence.

02

Tacit knowledge is invisible to existing systems

The enterprise content stack can only work with what has been externalised. The expertise in people's heads, the institutional memory embedded in practice, the relationships that determine how decisions really get made: none of these register in any current platform. They are the largest and most valuable part of the knowledge estate, and they are entirely unmeasured. APQC's 2024 research identified tacit knowledge capture as the most urgent priority for knowledge management teams precisely because the gap is now widely felt, even if it is not yet closed.APQC, 2024

03

Knowledge value is context-dependent

A piece of knowledge can be highly valuable in one context and irrelevant in another. Its value is also dynamic. It depreciates as conditions change, and appreciates as it becomes more applicable. Static asset valuation frameworks are not designed for assets whose value shifts continuously in response to context.

04

Confidence is absent from all current frameworks

Even where knowledge has been captured, no current framework distinguishes between what an organisation knows with high confidence and what it merely believes, assumes, or has recorded without validation. The consequences of this are no longer theoretical. In 2024, 47% of enterprise AI users reported making at least one major decision based on content that was later found to be inaccurate or hallucinated.Fullview.io / enterprise AI survey, 2024 All explicit knowledge is treated as equally credible, which means none of it is truly governed.

3 — Why Now

Three conditions that have converged to make this possible.

New categories do not emerge arbitrarily. They emerge when the need is clear, the infrastructure is mature, and the capability to act on it exists. All three conditions are now present for Knowledge Intelligence.

01

Content estates have reached meaningful scale

Large organisations now hold content estates of sufficient volume and variety that patterns within them are genuinely significant. The signal-to-noise challenge is no longer a niche concern. It is a primary operational and strategic condition, and the estate is large enough that intelligence extracted from it carries real strategic weight.

02

AI has made meaning extraction tractable at scale

For most of the history of content management, extracting meaning from large, heterogeneous content estates required prohibitive manual effort. That constraint has lifted. 78% of organisations now use AI in at least one business function,McKinsey, 2025 yet nearly two-thirds have not yet begun scaling it across the enterprise. The technology exists. What is missing is the governing intelligence layer that makes AI outputs trustworthy enough to act on.

03

Decision stakes have raised the bar for confidence

In an environment of accelerating change, the cost of acting on poorly understood or misaligned knowledge has increased significantly. 74% of organisations report struggling to achieve and scale AI value despite widespread adoption,BCG, 2024 and the root cause is consistently the same: inadequate data and knowledge foundations, not inadequate technology. Leaders increasingly need not just information, but justified confidence: an evidenced basis for action that can be traced, challenged, and defended.

4 — Content Intelligence

The first tractable layer and the foundation for what follows.

Given the quantification challenge, any practical path toward Knowledge Intelligence must begin with what is currently tractable: the explicit layer. Content Intelligence is the discipline of extracting meaning from an organisation's content estate at scale, assessing signal strength, evaluating reliability, and producing insight that can justify decisions rather than merely inform them.

It is not the whole answer. But it is the necessary first layer, and it represents a fundamentally different orientation from the platforms that currently manage explicit knowledge.

5 — Knowledge Intelligence

The capability that spans the full knowledge estate.

Content Intelligence is the foundational layer, but it operates only on what has been externalised. Knowledge Intelligence is the capability that spans both the explicit and tacit dimensions of organisational knowledge: governed, confidence-weighted, and designed to support decisions that depend on the full range of what an organisation knows.

Where Content Intelligence asks what a piece of content means, Knowledge Intelligence asks what the organisation actually knows, how confident it is in that knowledge, and how it is changing.

6 — The Missing Foundation

Knowledge Intelligence requires a common standard that does not yet exist.

The single most important prerequisite for Knowledge Intelligence is the absence of a Common Knowledge Asset Standard: a shared framework for classifying, measuring, and governing knowledge across its tacit and explicit forms.

Without such a standard, Knowledge Intelligence remains aspirational. Organisations cannot compare the value of a senior practitioner's expertise against a library of validated documents. They cannot measure the rate at which institutional memory is being lost. They cannot govern the confidence level of the knowledge their most consequential decisions depend on. The global knowledge management market is valued at approximately $885 billion today and is projected to reach $2.5 trillion by 2030.Research and Markets, 2024 Yet the fundamental question of what a knowledge asset actually is remains unanswered. A common standard is what makes the rest possible.

Toward a Common Knowledge Asset Standard

A Common Knowledge Asset Standard would establish the shared vocabulary, classification schema, and measurement principles that allow tacit and explicit knowledge to be treated as a unified, governable asset class. What follows is a preliminary sketch and the beginning of a framework, not its conclusion.

Classification
Asset type
Tacit or explicit; personal, relational, or institutional.
Domain
The strategic or operational area the knowledge pertains to.
Transferability
The degree to which the knowledge can be extracted, documented, and shared.
Reach
Whether the knowledge is held individually, by a team, or institutionally.
Measurement
Confidence level
How well-evidenced or validated the knowledge is, distinguishing belief from justified understanding.
Decay rate
How quickly the knowledge depreciates relative to its domain's rate of change.
Decision relevance
The degree to which the knowledge is actively applied to material decisions.
Concentration risk
Whether critical knowledge is held by too few people or in too few places.
Governance
Ownership
Who is accountable for the quality and currency of the knowledge asset.
Validation pathway
How the knowledge is tested, challenged, and confirmed as reliable.
Succession
What happens to the knowledge asset when its holder leaves, retires, or changes role.
Strategic alignment
Whether the knowledge asset remains relevant to current organisational priorities.
This framework is a starting point, not a conclusion. The development of a full Common Knowledge Asset Standard is an open undertaking, one that will require contribution from practitioners, researchers, and organisations willing to treat knowledge with the same rigour they apply to their financial and operational assets.
7 — Decision Cases

Five decisions that Knowledge Intelligence makes newly possible.

The value of Knowledge Intelligence becomes concrete when anchored to decisions that were previously unavailable, or available only through costly, episodic, and unreliable manual effort.

01
Strategic Timing

Detecting emerging themes before they are named

Themes are typically recognised only after formalisation. Knowledge Intelligence enables early visibility of persistent, converging signals across both the explicit content estate and tacit knowledge networks, allowing leaders to see change forming before it becomes obvious without being asked to react to noise.

Earlier, better-timed strategic awareness without overreaction.
02
Risk

Measuring concentration risk in the knowledge estate

When critical expertise is held by one person, or institutional memory exists nowhere but in the minds of a small team, the organisation carries knowledge concentration risk it cannot currently see or quantify. Nearly half of executives already recognise this: 48% agree that employees take critical procedural and strategic knowledge with them when they leave.KMWorld, 2024 A Common Knowledge Asset Standard makes this risk visible and governable before it materialises as a crisis.

Knowledge risk becomes a manageable condition, not an invisible one.
03
Concept Health

Evidence-based evolution of strategic concepts

Organisations accumulate frameworks and models that persist long after their usefulness declines. Knowledge Intelligence provides visibility into how concepts perform across the full knowledge estate, in documents, in practice, and in expert judgement, enabling deliberate and evidenced evolution rather than conceptual drift.

Concepts change based on evidence, not politics or fatigue.
04
Confidence

Governing confidence across the knowledge estate

No current framework distinguishes between what an organisation knows with high confidence and what it merely assumes. The consequences are significant: in 2024, 47% of enterprise AI users reported making at least one major decision based on inaccurate or hallucinated content,enterprise AI survey, 2024 and 75% of leaders say they do not trust their own data for decision-making.Dataversity / Gartner, 2025 Knowledge Intelligence makes confidence a first-class governance dimension, ensuring the knowledge underpinning high-stakes decisions can be interrogated, not assumed.

Confidence becomes governable. Assumptions become visible.
05
External Alignment

Deliberate alignment between internal knowledge and external context

Internal knowledge, both explicit and tacit, drifts relative to external reality. Knowledge Intelligence treats external context as weighted evidence, allowing internal understanding to be reinforced, challenged, or recalibrated deliberately rather than by crisis. This applies to documented knowledge and to the assumptions embedded in expert practice.

Strategic choices grounded in evidenced alignment, not assumption.
8 — What Leadership Must Do

Three orientations that cannot be delegated.

Knowledge Intelligence is a strategic capability, which means it requires strategic ownership. Three shifts in orientation are necessary for it to take root and compound over time.

Knowledge as asset

Organisations must begin treating knowledge in all its forms, tacit and explicit, as a strategic asset with a measurable state, a trajectory, and a governance obligation. This means investing in the standards and frameworks that make that measurement possible.

Confidence governance

Governance must extend beyond compliance to cover the quality of understanding, whether the knowledge being relied upon is current, validated, and evidenced. Confidence must become a first-class governance dimension with ownership, accountability, and measurement at the executive level.

Trajectories

The most valuable intelligence is not what the current state is, but what has changed and why. Leaders must invest in the infrastructure of understanding over time, including the ability to see how the organisation's knowledge estate is evolving, where it is concentrating, and where it is at risk of decay.

Closing Statement

The category is ready to be defined. The standard is ready to be built.

The enterprise has built extraordinary capability for managing explicit knowledge. That infrastructure is valuable and will remain so. What it has not yet built is the capability to treat everything an organisation knows, tacit and explicit together, as a unified, governable, and measurable asset class.

That capability is Knowledge Intelligence. Its prerequisite is a Common Knowledge Asset Standard that does not yet exist, but whose development is now both necessary and possible.

The conditions have converged. The content estates are large enough. The AI capability is sufficient. The decision stakes are high enough. The cost of unmeasured knowledge is no longer acceptable.

This paper is a founding statement, not a final answer. The work of defining the standard, building the capability, and proving its value is what comes next.
