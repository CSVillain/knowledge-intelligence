Opening Statement

Every major asset class has a framework. Knowledge has never had one.

Financial assets can be measured, compared, and governed because accounting standards exist that make this possible. Physical assets — machinery, property, infrastructure — are valued, depreciated, and insured according to shared frameworks. Even data is increasingly treated as a governable asset class, with emerging standards for ownership, quality, and provenance.

Knowledge has no equivalent. Despite being the asset on which most consequential decisions depend, knowledge has never been defined carefully enough to be measured, governed, or strategically managed as a unified class. Organisations know this intuitively. They speak of knowledge as critical, irreplaceable, and at risk. Yet they have no common framework for determining what it is, what it is worth, or what happens when it degrades.

I believe this absence is not incidental. It is the central unsolved problem of the knowledge economy, and the reason a new capability has not yet been possible to define, let alone deliver.

That capability is Knowledge Intelligence: the ability to treat everything an organisation knows as a governable, measurable, and decision-relevant asset. Tacit and explicit. Human and documented. Relational and institutional. It has no established category, no recognised framework, and no dominant platform. This paper is its founding statement.

Delivering Knowledge Intelligence requires a coherent framework — a way of classifying knowledge across its forms, measuring its confidence and decay, and governing it with the same care applied to financial or operational assets. This paper defines what that framework looks like and why building this capability is now both necessary and possible.

1 — Knowledge as an Asset Class

Knowledge is broader, deeper, and harder to measure than content alone.

Every organisation knows that knowledge matters. What none of them have developed is a coherent, shared understanding of what knowledge actually is as an asset — in the full sense of the word. Before any intelligence framework can be built, that foundation has to be established.

Knowledge exists in two distinct but inseparable forms. Neither is sufficient on its own. Both need to be accounted for.

Knowledge expressed in words and numbers represents only the tip of the iceberg. The larger part is tacit: highly personal, hard to formalise, and deeply rooted in action, experience, and individual values.

Ikujiro Nonaka and Hirotaka Takeuchi — The Knowledge-Creating Company, Oxford University Press, 1995
Tacit Knowledge

What organisations know but cannot easily say

Tacit knowledge is the most valuable and the least visible form. It cannot be directly extracted, stored, or transferred the way a document can. It lives in people, in relationships, in culture, and in practice. Research consistently estimates that tacit knowledge accounts for approximately 80% of an organisation's total knowledge base.Nonaka & Takeuchi

Human expertise and judgement
The ability to make sound decisions in complex situations, built through experience and almost impossible to fully put into words.
Relationships and networks
Who trusts whom, who knows whom, and the informal channels through which real decisions are made and real knowledge flows.
Process and institutional memory
Why things are done the way they are done. The accumulated context behind conventions that are rarely written down and often lost when people leave.
Experience and pattern recognition
The ability to recognise situations, anticipate outcomes, and navigate ambiguity based on having navigated it before.
Explicit Knowledge

What organisations know and have expressed

Explicit knowledge is what has been written down and captured — in content, data, and structured form. It is legible, transferable, and the primary focus of the existing enterprise content stack. Yet even this visible layer presents significant governance challenges at scale.

Documents and records
Policies, reports, research, contracts, guidance, and the full range of structured written output produced by the organisation.
Structured data and signals
Quantitative records, system outputs, and operational data that encode organisational activity and performance.
Frameworks and models
The conceptual structures (taxonomies, methodologies, principles) through which the organisation understands and describes itself.
Captured expertise
Explicit articulations of tacit knowledge including playbooks, case studies, and lessons learned, where the organisation has attempted to make expertise transferable.

Treating only explicit knowledge as a measurable asset is like managing a company's finances by counting only the cash on hand. Technically legible, but strategically blind.

2 — The Quantification Challenge

Why knowledge has resisted being treated as an asset until now.

If knowledge is so clearly valuable, why has no common framework for measuring and governing it yet emerged? The answer is not lack of interest. Knowledge — particularly in its tacit forms — presents genuinely hard problems that existing frameworks have not solved. The cost of that gap is well documented, even if the solutions remain absent.

01

No common unit of measure

Financial assets have currency. Physical assets have weight, volume, and condition. Knowledge has no agreed unit. A decade of domain expertise cannot be straightforwardly compared to a thousand documents. Without a common unit, comparison, governance, and investment decisions become matters of opinion rather than evidence.

02

Tacit knowledge is invisible to existing systems

The enterprise content stack can only work with what has been written down and captured. The expertise in people's heads, the institutional memory embedded in practice, the relationships that determine how decisions really get made: none of these show up in any current platform. They are the largest and most valuable part of the knowledge estate, and they are entirely unmeasured. APQC's 2024 research identified tacit knowledge capture as the most urgent priority for knowledge management teams precisely because the gap is now widely felt, even if it is not yet closed.APQC, 2024

03

Knowledge value is context-dependent

A piece of knowledge can be highly valuable in one context and irrelevant in another. Its value is also dynamic. It depreciates as conditions change, and appreciates as it becomes more applicable. Static asset valuation frameworks are not designed for assets whose value shifts continuously in response to context.

04

Confidence is absent from all current frameworks

Even where knowledge has been captured, no current framework distinguishes between what an organisation knows with high confidence and what it merely believes, assumes, or has recorded without validation. The consequences of this are no longer theoretical. In 2024, 47% of enterprise AI users reported making at least one major decision based on content that was later found to be inaccurate or hallucinated.Fullview.io / enterprise AI survey, 2024 All explicit knowledge is treated as equally credible, which means none of it is truly governed.

3 — Why Now

Three conditions that have converged to make this possible.

New categories don't emerge arbitrarily. They emerge when the need is clear, the infrastructure is ready, and the capability to act on it exists. All three are now in place for Knowledge Intelligence.

01

Content estates have reached meaningful scale

Large organisations now hold content estates of sufficient volume and variety that patterns within them are genuinely significant. The signal-to-noise challenge is no longer a niche concern. It is a primary operational and strategic condition, and the estate is large enough that intelligence extracted from it carries real strategic weight.

02

AI has made meaning extraction possible at scale

For most of the history of content management, extracting meaning from large, varied content estates required an enormous amount of manual effort. That constraint has lifted. 78% of organisations now use AI in at least one business function,McKinsey, 2025 yet nearly two-thirds have not yet begun scaling it across the enterprise. The technology exists. What is missing is the governing intelligence layer that makes AI outputs trustworthy enough to act on.

03

Decision stakes have raised the bar for confidence

In an environment of accelerating change, the cost of acting on poorly understood or misaligned knowledge has increased significantly. 74% of organisations report struggling to achieve and scale AI value despite widespread adoption,BCG, 2024 and the root cause is consistently the same: inadequate data and knowledge foundations, not inadequate technology. Leaders increasingly need not just information, but justified confidence: an evidenced basis for action that can be traced, challenged, and defended.

Condition 01 — Scale
Content estates are large enough
The volume and variety of content now held by large organisations makes intelligence extraction genuinely significant — not a niche experiment.
Condition 02 — Capability
AI can extract meaning at scale
The manual effort once required to process knowledge estates at this scale has been removed. The technology capability now exists to do what previously couldn't be done.
Condition 03 — Stakes
The cost of not knowing is too high
Accelerating change and AI adoption have raised the cost of operating on poorly understood knowledge. Justified confidence is now a strategic requirement, not a nice-to-have.
All three conditions have converged. The moment for Knowledge Intelligence is now.
4 — Content Intelligence

The first layer we can act on — and the foundation for what follows.

Given the challenges above, any practical path toward Knowledge Intelligence has to start with what we can act on right now: the explicit layer. Content Intelligence is the discipline of extracting meaning from an organisation's content estate at scale, assessing signal strength, evaluating reliability, and producing insight that can justify decisions rather than merely inform them.

It is not the whole answer. But it is the necessary first layer, and it represents a fundamentally different way of thinking about the platforms that currently manage explicit knowledge.

5 — Knowledge Intelligence

The capability that spans the full knowledge estate.

Content Intelligence is the foundational layer, but it only works with what has been written down. Knowledge Intelligence is the capability that spans both the explicit and tacit dimensions of organisational knowledge: governed, confidence-weighted, and designed to support decisions that depend on the full range of what an organisation knows.

Where Content Intelligence asks what a piece of content means, Knowledge Intelligence asks what the organisation actually knows, how confident it is in that knowledge, and how it is changing. The distinction matters. An organisation can have excellent content management and still be operating on outdated assumptions, losing critical expertise through attrition, or failing to connect what it knows in one part of the business to decisions being made in another.

Knowledge Intelligence addresses this by treating the full knowledge estate — documented and undocumented, verified and assumed, current and decaying — as a single governed system. Every knowledge asset carries a record of what it is, where it came from, how reliable it is, and how much it is actually being used. That record is not static. It updates as the asset ages, as new evidence emerges, as the asset is cited or contradicted, and as the people who hold related knowledge change.

The result is an intelligence layer that can answer questions no current platform can. Not just "where is the document?" but "how much should we trust it?" Not just "who is the expert?" but "what happens to that expertise when they leave?" Not just "what do we know?" but "how confident are we, and where are the gaps?"

6 — The Knowledge Intelligence Framework

How Knowledge Intelligence actually works.

Knowledge Intelligence is not a philosophy — it is a capability with a defined architecture. At its core is a deceptively simple idea: every piece of knowledge an organisation holds should exist as a governed asset with a known identity, a measurable quality, and a traceable history. Not just documents. Not just data. Everything the organisation knows, in whatever form it takes.

This is what a Common Knowledge Asset is. A structured record that travels with every piece of knowledge through its entire lifecycle — from the moment it enters the system to the moment it is superseded, retired, or lost. It captures not just what the knowledge is, but where it came from, how reliable it is, who has used it, what it has influenced, and how its value is changing over time.

The global knowledge management market is valued at approximately $885 billion today and is projected to reach $2.5 trillion by 2030.Research and Markets, 2024 Yet the vast majority of that investment produces systems that store and retrieve knowledge, not systems that understand and govern it. The Common Knowledge Asset is what changes that — turning passive repositories into active intelligence.

The Common Knowledge Asset

Every knowledge asset within a Knowledge Intelligence framework carries a structured record across three dimensions. Together, these dimensions give the organisation a complete, continuously updated picture of what it knows and what that knowledge is worth.

Classification
Asset type
Tacit or explicit; personal, relational, or institutional.
Domain
The strategic or operational area the knowledge pertains to.
Transferability
The degree to which the knowledge can be extracted, documented, and shared.
Reach
Whether the knowledge is held individually, by a team, or institutionally.
Measurement
Confidence level
How well-evidenced or validated the knowledge is, distinguishing belief from justified understanding.
Decay rate
How quickly the knowledge depreciates relative to its domain's rate of change.
Decision relevance
The degree to which the knowledge is actively applied to material decisions.
Concentration risk
Whether critical knowledge is held by too few people or in too few places.
Governance
Ownership
Who is accountable for the quality and currency of the knowledge asset.
Validation pathway
How the knowledge is tested, challenged, and confirmed as reliable.
Succession
What happens to the knowledge asset when its holder leaves, retires, or changes role.
Strategic alignment
Whether the knowledge asset remains relevant to current organisational priorities.
These three dimensions work together to produce a single composite measure: the Knowledge Health Score. A continuously updated indicator of an asset's fitness for use, combining quality, value, and lineage into one signal that tells the organisation whether to trust, review, or retire what it knows.
7 — Decision Cases

Five decisions that Knowledge Intelligence makes newly possible.

The value of Knowledge Intelligence becomes concrete when anchored to decisions that were previously unavailable, or available only through costly, one-off, and unreliable manual effort.

01
Strategic Timing

Detecting emerging themes before they are named

Themes are typically only recognised once someone names them. Knowledge Intelligence enables early visibility of persistent, converging signals across both the documented content estate and tacit knowledge networks, allowing leaders to see change forming before it becomes obvious — without being asked to react to noise.

Earlier, better-timed strategic awareness without overreaction.
02
Risk

Measuring concentration risk in the knowledge estate

When critical expertise is held by one person, or institutional memory exists nowhere but in the minds of a small team, the organisation is carrying knowledge risk it cannot currently see or measure. Nearly half of executives already recognise this: 48% agree that employees take critical procedural and strategic knowledge with them when they leave.KMWorld, 2024 Knowledge Intelligence makes this risk visible and measurable before it becomes a crisis — treating knowledge concentration the same way a finance team treats exposure in a portfolio.

Knowledge risk becomes a manageable condition, not an invisible one.
03
Concept Health

Evidence-based evolution of strategic concepts

Organisations accumulate frameworks and models that persist long after their usefulness declines. Knowledge Intelligence provides visibility into how concepts perform across the full knowledge estate, in documents, in practice, and in expert judgement, enabling deliberate and evidenced evolution rather than conceptual drift.

Concepts change based on evidence, not politics or fatigue.
04
Confidence

Governing confidence across the knowledge estate

No current framework distinguishes between what an organisation knows with high confidence and what it merely assumes. The consequences are significant: in 2024, 47% of enterprise AI users reported making at least one major decision based on inaccurate or hallucinated content,enterprise AI survey, 2024 and 75% of leaders say they do not trust their own data for decision-making.Dataversity / Gartner, 2025 Knowledge Intelligence makes confidence a first-class governance dimension, ensuring the knowledge underpinning high-stakes decisions can be interrogated, not assumed.

Confidence becomes governable. Assumptions become visible.
05
External Alignment

Deliberate alignment between internal knowledge and external context

Internal knowledge — both explicit and tacit — drifts relative to external reality. Knowledge Intelligence treats external context as weighted evidence, allowing internal understanding to be reinforced, challenged, or updated deliberately rather than by crisis. This applies to documented knowledge and to the assumptions embedded in expert practice.

Strategic choices grounded in real alignment, not assumption.

Closing Statement

The capability is ready to be built. The moment is now.

Organisations have built extraordinary capability for managing explicit knowledge. That infrastructure is valuable and will remain so. What hasn't been built yet is the capability to treat everything an organisation knows — tacit and explicit together — as a unified, governable, and measurable asset class.

That capability is Knowledge Intelligence. It is not a product category that already exists under a different name. It is not a feature of current platforms waiting to be unlocked. It is a genuinely new capability — one that requires a different way of thinking about what knowledge is, what it is worth, and what it means to govern it well.

The conditions have converged. Content estates are large enough. AI capability is sufficient. The decision stakes are high enough. The cost of unmeasured knowledge is no longer acceptable.

This paper is a founding statement, not a final answer. The work of defining the capability, building the framework, and proving its value is what comes next — and it's work I intend to do.
